# 🌍 US Emissions Breakdown Analysis (Databricks + SQL)

An end-to-end data analysis project exploring **energy consumption and greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions across U.S. counties**.  
This project leverages the 2023 dataset from the **U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)** to uncover regional disparities, efficiency patterns, and key emission drivers.

## 🧰 Tech Stack

- **Databricks** – Data processing & interactive dashboard development  
- **SQL** – Data cleaning, transformation, and aggregation  
- **AI-assisted analysis** – Query optimization and insight exploration  
- **Data Visualization** – Interactive dashboards (Databricks)

## 📊 Project Objective

To analyze U.S. emissions at the county level and answer key questions such as:

- Where are emissions most concentrated?
- How does population influence per capita emissions?
- What drives differences in energy consumption and emissions efficiency across regions?

## 🔍 Key Insights

### 1. Emissions by Region
Central and Eastern U.S. regions exhibit significantly higher emissions compared to Western regions, indicating strong geographic and industrial disparities.

### 2. Per Capita Emissions vs Population
Counties with larger populations tend to have lower per capita greenhouse gas emissions, suggesting potential efficiency gains in densely populated areas.

### 3. Top 10 Highest-Emitting States
Texas ranks as the highest-emitting state, contributing approximately **20.6% of total emissions** among the top 10 states.

### 4. Top 10 Counties by Total Emissions
Maricopa County (Arizona) records the highest total emissions at approximately **9.81 million metric tons of CO₂e**, highlighting the impact of large metropolitan regions.

### 5. Energy Consumption vs Emissions
Higher energy consumption is generally associated with higher emissions; however, variations across counties suggest differences in **energy efficiency and energy mix composition**.

### 6. Top 10 Counties by Energy Consumption
Harris County (Texas) shows the highest energy consumption across all U.S. counties, reflecting its large industrial and population base.

## 💡 Key Takeaways

- Emissions are highly concentrated in a small number of states and counties.
- Population density plays a key role in shaping per capita emissions efficiency.
- Energy consumption alone does not fully explain emissions differences—energy efficiency and energy sources vary significantly across regions.
- Transportation and industrial activity are key underlying drivers of emissions patterns.
